1) Crossword / grid conventions

Leverage how “Down” works in crosswords themselves.

e.g., clues that blur Down (direction in grid) vs geographic south

Why interesting:
Meta-layer ambiguity; could create a pivot between puzzle terminology and real-world direction.

2) Typography / layout orientation

“Down” vs “below” vs “lower” in page layout or UI.

e.g., something that initially reads as screen/UI positioning

Potential trap:
Solver thinks visual layout, then shifts to geographic meaning.

3) Sports terminology

“Down” (football), “southpaw” (boxing), “going south” (slang decline)

especially cases where “down” has a non-directional meaning

Why useful:
Strong initial parse unrelated to geography → pivot

4) Finance / trends

“Going south” = declining

markets, stocks, performance

Potential:
Start with metaphorical “south” → force literal directional reinterpretation

5) Weather / climate framing

“Down south,” “southern winds,” “cold front moving south”

Twist opportunity:
Weather language vs literal movement direction

6) Aviation / navigation jargon (non-compass)
flight paths, headings, runways, ATC phrasing

Why distinct:
Different mental model than compass (procedural vs geometric)

7) Migration / biology
birds flying south, seasonal movement

Trap:
Species names vs direction vs behavior

8) Cultural idioms / slang
“go south,” “down south,” “head south”

Angle:
Exploit ambiguity between:

idiomatic meaning (decline / region)
literal directional meaning
9) Relative perspective / frame-of-reference shifts
“down” depends on viewpoint (map vs globe vs person)

Why high ceiling:
This is more abstract but can produce clean pivots if grounded well

10) Word formation / morphology tricks
“-ward” suffix vs base word
contrast with:
south
southwards
downward

Potential:
Exploit form-based expectations (solver fills SOUTH, needs SOUTHWARD)

Meta-evaluation

Compared to the previous five groups, these are:

Less geography-centric
More about:
language use
metaphor vs literal meaning
domain shifts (sports, finance, UI, etc.)
Pushback (important)

A lot of these:

have high creative potential
but also high failure risk

The danger is:

generating clever ideas that don’t translate into
a credible solver experience

In particular:

finance / slang / metaphor categories often fail the literal resolution check
crossword/meta clues risk feeling gimmicky
